# Experience Plan

Status: Active direction for the next iteration  
Last updated: 17 July 2026

This document owns the staged story, animation, and interaction plan for Forms of Intelligence. `PRODUCT.md` owns the purpose and audience. `DESIGN.md` owns the current visual system. Source code and tests own implementation facts.

## Goal

Turn the current sequence of strong individual chapters into one continuous transformation in which the reader helps determine what intelligence becomes.

The experience should leave the reader with three ideas:

1. Intelligence has always had many forms: cellular, embodied, collective, animal, human, and machine.
2. Machine intelligence belongs to a physical and human lineage. It begins in matter and inherits patterns made by people.
3. Capability alone does not create meaning. What we ask intelligence to become—and what we choose to preserve—shapes the result.

## What remains strong

- The opening question and immediate tactile field.
- The near-black science-theatre setting and distinct cobalt, green, coral, and yellow roles.
- The biological specimen image and the scale of the chapter typography.
- Optional narration and visible chapter progress.
- The closing emphasis on conversation rather than replacement.

These are foundations to develop, not elements to replace without evidence.

## Main shortcomings in the current prototype

1. The chapters sit beside one another more than they transform into one another.
2. Human intelligence appears mainly at the end, even though human language, art, mathematics, tools, and code are the source material for machine intelligence.
3. The particle field represents too many kinds of intelligence with similar physics.
4. Touch bends the same field in most chapters instead of expressing the idea of each chapter.
5. The critique of disposable AI output is stated but not experienced.
6. The final touch changes the field briefly but the system does not remember what the reader contributed.

## Target story

### Prologue — The question

The field asks: What is the shape of intelligence? The first touch does not open a menu. It changes the matter on screen and establishes that the reader is part of the experiment.

### Act I — Matter holds a pattern

Sand pours, gathers, melts, and crystallises. A grain becomes silicon. Silicon becomes an etched path. A pulse enters that path. Matter begins to store and transform a pattern.

The central line is: **We taught stone to remember.**

### Act II — Intelligence grows, then leaves the skull

The electrical pulse becomes a neural impulse. Cells sense, DNA copies, roots seek water, and nervous systems learn what to keep.

Human intelligence then gains its own movement. A hand becomes a gesture. The gesture becomes a mark, writing, musical notation, mathematics, a tool, code, and finally a structure a machine can execute. The purpose is not to place humanity at the top of a ladder. It is to show how human thought learned to live outside the body long before modern AI.

The central line is: **The mind escaped the skull before the machine arrived.**

### Act III — Minds unlike ours

The essay moves through a small number of concrete examples instead of a list:

- An ant colony remembers through trails and local decisions.
- A dog encounters a body as a changing chemical atmosphere.
- An animal such as an octopus, bat, shark, or migratory bird demonstrates a distributed or unfamiliar sense.

Each example earns a different visual system and interaction. The reader should understand the intelligence through behaviour before explanatory copy appears.

The central line is: **One mind is not always one body, and one world is not always visible to us.**

### Act IV — Machines become alien

Machine intelligence combines senses, scales, and forms that no single organism possesses. “Beyond superhuman” does not mean a higher score on a human test. It means magnetic weather, chemical traces, deep time, distributed attention, or patterns spanning many bodies.

The reader changes the machine's available sense and watches the same world become a different problem.

The central line is: **Beyond superhuman is not smarter. It is stranger.**

### Act V — Abundance without intention

This is the emotional low point. One gesture produces something surprising. Repetition produces endless variations. The system accelerates, anticipates, clones, fills the screen, and loses coherence. Abundance becomes sameness.

The reader cannot fix this by requesting more. They must slow the system, choose what matters, and remove what does not. The recovery turns the argument about AI “slop” into an experience.

The central line is: **Magic disappears when creation has no attention, resistance, or reason.**

### Finale — Teach it one thing

The reader draws, taps a rhythm, or optionally speaks a short sound. The system remembers that contribution locally and uses it to form the final image. The gesture reappears as a neural branch, street plan, swarm trail, or melody.

The final exchange is:

> This is not the shape of intelligence.  
> It is the shape you asked it to take.

## Continuity rule

Every transition must preserve one visible trace from the previous movement.

| From | Preserved trace | Into |
|---|---|---|
| Sand | One grain | Silicon lattice node |
| Silicon | Electrical pulse | Neural impulse |
| Neuron | Branching path | Human mark or written line |
| Human mark | Repeated stroke | Ant pheromone trail |
| Pheromone | Chemical plume | Machine scent map |
| Scent map | Contour | Alien field line |
| Generated variation | One chosen feature | Final remembered form |

The connecting particle field may carry these traces, but it must not be the finished visual language for every chapter.

## Visual behaviour by form

| Form | Material behaviour | Visual character |
|---|---|---|
| Sand | Pour, pile, scatter, compress | Granular, heavy, warm, noisy |
| Silicon | Snap, align, etch, pulse | Precise, planar, cold, abrupt |
| Biological | Grow, branch, prune, repair | Wet, fibrous, translucent, asymmetric |
| Human | Gesture, annotate, repeat, compose | Intentional marks, tools, notation, rhythm |
| Swarm | Follow, diverge, leave trails | Many local agents, no central body |
| Chemical | Diffuse, mix, fade, bind | Volumetric plumes and molecular fingerprints |
| Alien sense | Bend, resonate, accumulate | Field lines, topology, unfamiliar scales |
| Generative system | Morph, clone, accelerate, collapse | Seductive abundance becoming visual noise |

Typography should participate in these behaviours. It should not use the same large static composition in every movement. Questions may remain monumental; evidence and discovery can become quieter, more spatial, or kinetic.

## Interaction language

Each movement gets one primary verb. All interactions need a single-touch fallback and a keyboard equivalent where practical.

| Movement | Primary interaction | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Press and hold | Establish a relationship with the field |
| Matter | Pinch or compress | Force loose grains into ordered silicon |
| Human lineage | Trace | Turn gesture into stored notation |
| Swarm | Draw a path | Create a trail that local agents may adopt |
| Chemical sense | Drag or optionally blow | Disturb a plume and reveal its composition |
| Alien sense | Tilt or choose a sense | Change what the machine can perceive |
| Crisis | Hold, select, remove | Resist speed and preserve one meaningful form |
| Finale | Draw, tap, or optionally speak | Teach the system something it can remember |

No microphone, motion-sensor, or orientation permission may be required to complete the essay. Permission-dependent inputs are enhancements with visible touch alternatives.

## Voice and sound

Voice should become part of the encounter rather than only a reading mode.

- The field may ask the opening questions aloud after the first deliberate touch.
- Biological intelligence can use layered pulses, breath, and overlapping fragments.
- The swarm communicates through rhythm rather than a human sentence.
- Chemical sensing uses airy noise, crackle, and changes in density.
- Machine voice gradually assembles material from earlier movements.
- Captions and full written copy remain available at all times.
- Device text-to-speech remains a fallback until authored narration exists.

Sound must begin only after user input, have a persistent mute control, and add no required information.

## Implementation sequence

The sequence follows one rule: prove one representative transformation before spreading a new technique through the essay.

### Phase 1 — Continuous matter-to-life transformation

Objective: prove that three movements can feel like one uninterrupted change.

Build:

- Replace discrete scene switching for sand, silicon, and the first neural form with continuous progress.
- Preserve one grain as it becomes a lattice node and then an electrical impulse.
- Give sand and silicon visibly different physics.
- Tie the headline and explanatory copy to the visual transition without hiding readable defaults.
- Keep the existing chapter navigation and reduced-motion fallback working.

Exit criteria:

- The transition reads clearly with the copy hidden.
- Touch affects loose sand differently from ordered silicon.
- Scrolling in either direction reverses the transformation cleanly.
- Direct checks pass at 1366×1024 and 1024×1366 with no overflow or browser errors.
- The reduced-motion version presents three clear static states with a simple crossfade.

### Phase 2 — Human intelligence lineage

Objective: make human intelligence an active source rather than a closing qualification.

Build:

- Add the gesture → mark → language → notation → mathematics → code sequence.
- Let the reader trace the initial gesture.
- Carry that gesture into the next movement as stored information.
- Rewrite adjacent copy so the new movement changes the argument instead of repeating it.

Exit criteria:

- A reader can explain how human thought moved outside the body before reaching silicon.
- The interaction has a clear result without instructions longer than one sentence.
- The chapter retains human warmth without becoming nostalgic or treating humanity as the final form.

### Phase 3 — Distinct animal and alien systems

Objective: make unfamiliar intelligence legible through behaviour.

Build:

- Create a pheromone-trail swarm engine.
- Create a chemical plume and scent-map engine.
- Create at least one non-visual sense such as magnetic fields, echolocation, or pressure.
- Give each system a different interaction verb and sound material.

Exit criteria:

- The three systems remain distinguishable in silhouette and motion without relying on color.
- Their interactions are understandable before explanatory text appears.
- Each system meets the same performance, keyboard, touch, and reduced-motion checks.

### Phase 4 — The abundance crisis

Objective: let the reader feel why generative abundance can become disposable.

Build:

- Start with one meaningful transformation.
- Accelerate variation and anticipation through repeated input.
- Let visual coherence collapse without causing flashing or inaccessible motion.
- Give the reader a deliberate preservation and removal action.
- Restore quiet around the selected form.

Exit criteria:

- The emotional change is apparent without the explanatory paragraph.
- The recovery action is discovered or taught at the moment it becomes necessary.
- The sequence cannot trap the reader in a high-motion state.

### Phase 5 — Remembered finale

Objective: make the closing argument depend on the reader's contribution.

Build:

- Capture one drawing or tap rhythm with an optional voice input.
- Store it only in the current browser session unless a later decision changes that policy.
- Reuse its structure in the final form and earlier visual languages.
- Provide a clear reset and replay action.

Exit criteria:

- Two readers produce visibly different endings.
- The remembered contribution is recognisable without being copied literally.
- Refresh, reset, and permission-denied paths behave predictably.

### Phase 6 — Authored voice, sound, accessibility, and performance

Objective: make the complete experience coherent and ready for broader testing.

Build:

- Replace generic narration with authored audio if production resources allow.
- Compose a restrained generative sound system tied to material behaviour.
- Art-direct reduced-motion states rather than merely freezing the animated version.
- Audit focus order, contrast, narration controls, captions, zoom, and touch targets.
- Profile canvas work, image loading, battery use, and memory at iPad-sized viewports.

Exit criteria:

- The full documented check passes.
- Every required idea is available without sound, motion, color, microphone, or device sensors.
- Representative readers can complete the essay and describe its central argument before promotion is considered.

## Decisions already made

- Build as an iPad-first web prototype until the experience proves a reason to become native.
- Do not introduce a chatbox.
- Keep the near-black environment and the current named color roles.
- Use the particle field as connective matter, not as the universal visual answer.
- Do not add more examples until the first continuous transformation works.
- Treat generated raster imagery as provisional project material.
- Keep optional sensing inputs local and permission-aware.

## Decisions to make when they become relevant

- Whether authored narration uses one voice or several distinct voices.
- Which third animal sense joins swarm and scent.
- Whether microphone and device orientation materially improve the experience after touch fallbacks work.
- Whether the final remembered form should survive a refresh.
- What scientific review and source notes are required before public release.
- Whether the interaction quality justifies a native iPad build using Metal and Core Haptics.

## Next implementation task

Begin Phase 1 with a representative slice: refactor the current particle renderer to accept continuous story progress, then build the reversible sand → silicon → neural impulse transformation. Preserve the existing opening, navigation, narration, biological image, and remaining chapter states while this slice is proved.
